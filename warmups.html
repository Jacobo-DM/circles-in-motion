Commons Draft

This page is a living document. RageDance warm-ups are shared in the spirit of the commons: no gatekeepers, no perfection, only practices that help us arrive safely in our bodies.

These warm-ups do not replace medical advice or the wisdom of experienced trainers and physical therapists. They are simple, time-tested ways to wake up the joints, the breath, and the nervous system using what you already have: your own attention.

Safety Principles

  • Sharp pain = stop. Pause immediately. Reassess angle, speed, posture, and range.
  • Good discomfort = go gently. A mild, stretchy feeling can be fine. Forcing or grinding is not.
  • No “powering through.” RageDance is not a punishment practice. We do not prove worth through pain.
  • Warm-up is part of the practice, not a chore. It is the doorway to safe power and depth.
  • Breathe. If you find yourself holding your breath, the practice is already too hard. Soften.

Always adapt movements to your own body, your history of injury, and your current energy level. If you are unsure, scale down the intensity and range.

1. Shoulder + Wrist Activation (Circle Expansion)

Purpose

To gently lubricate the wrist, elbow, and shoulder joints; wake up stabilizing muscles; and prepare safely for elbow strikes or other fast, loaded movements.

How

  • Stand tall with feet about hip–shoulder width apart.
  • Extend both arms out to the sides at shoulder height (the “scarecrow line”).
  • Begin with small wrist circles, in both directions, keeping the arms long and soft.
  • Add the fingers: spread wide, curl in, play with opening and closing as you circle the wrists.
  • Notice how the movement ripples up the forearm into the shoulder.
  • Start to draw tiny circles with the whole arm, as if the fingertips were tracing coins in the air.
  • Slowly make the circles larger, staying relaxed and curious rather than tense.
  • Explore directions: forward, backward, slightly in front of you, slightly behind you.

Why it helps

  • Gentle circular motion encourages synovial fluid to move through the joints (natural lubrication).
  • Small-to-large circles recruit the rotator cuff and other stabilizers before you ask them to handle impact.
  • The nervous system learns to trust a range of motion before you add speed or power.
  • Within 30–60 seconds, many people feel warmer chest, shoulders, and lungs—an excellent prelude to RageDance.

If your shoulders are tender, keep the circles small and lower than shoulder height. Consistency is more important than size.

2. One-Arm Infinity Loop (Circle Flow)

Purpose

To restore shoulder mobility, reveal tight spots, and train whole-body integration. Slow at first, it can eventually become a defensive movement when done with speed and control.

How

  • Stand with feet shoulder-width apart.
  • Place one hand (for example, the left) on your waist or hip for grounding.
  • Let the other arm (for example, the right) hang relaxed, then begin to draw a large circle:
    • Up in front of the body,
    • Out to the side,
    • Then around and back, completing the loop.
  • Move slowly enough that you can hear and feel what is happening in the joint: pops, crackles, and shifts.
  • If you feel sharp pain, stop and make the circle smaller or change the angle.
  • As the movement becomes smooth and comfortable, reverse the direction of the circle.
  • Next layer: allow the feet and hips to join in. Let your weight shift. Let the heel lift. Let the body follow the arm.
  • Advanced: gently increase the speed without losing softness or control, noticing how the path of the arm could deflect or redirect a strike.

Why it helps

  • Slow circular loading hydrates fascia and helps “unstick” tissues around the shoulder.
  • Adding the feet and hips brings the entire kinetic chain online, from the ground up.
  • Reversing direction balances strength and coordination on less-used lines of movement.
  • It naturally reveals limitations: where crackles appear, where range disappears, where breath shortens.

Treat this as a “listening practice.” You are not trying to force your arm into a bigger circle; you are inviting the body to show you what is available today.

3. Stop–Breath Reset (Pulse Recalibration)

Purpose

To prevent overwhelm, teach self-interruption, and remind the body that you can always return to neutral—even in the middle of intense movement.

How

  • In the middle of a RageDance sequence—even mid-swing—simply stop.
  • Return to your relaxed stance: feet grounded, knees soft, jaw unclenched.
  • Take 1–3 slow breaths, feeling the ribs expand and soften.
  • Notice your heart rate, your thoughts, your emotional temperature.
  • Only resume movement when your breath feels steady again.

Why it helps

  • Teaches the nervous system that intensity does not have to escalate into fight–flight.
  • Reinforces agency: you set the tempo of your practice.
  • Builds the habit of checking in instead of pushing through.

You can use this reset any time you feel overwhelmed, disconnected, or tempted to “prove” something with your movement.

4. The Play Gate

Purpose

To restore permission to play. To explore your own way of moving instead of copying someone else. To keep RageDance honest, alive, and responsive to the day you are actually having.

How

  • Begin by asking: What does my body need today?
  • Allow yourself to move in ways that might look “wrong” or “silly” from the outside.
  • Let your practice drift between moods:
    • More dance,
    • More martial,
    • More mindfulness or quiet presence.
  • Let it change with seasons, with the group you are in, with the music or the silence.
  • Notice any urge to imitate; gently return to your own curiosity instead.

Why it helps

  • Play reduces internal threat and makes learning faster and safer.
  • It dissolves perfectionism and performance anxiety—major sources of tension and injury.
  • It aligns RageDance with Discovery Mode: exploration over performance.

You can make the Play Gate its own small ritual: one song, one minute, or even one breath where the only rule is that you are allowed to be fully yourself.
